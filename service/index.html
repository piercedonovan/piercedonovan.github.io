Service

Facilitating Student Welfare and Inclusion

As an undergraduate student, I found college to be stressful. I still do. In my interactions with undergraduates, I encourage them to talk to me when they feel overwhelmed. I’ve attended several mental health training seminars to maximize the help I can provide. When I recognize the need for a professional, I play an active role in referring students to therapy and advising services. It is comforting to be able to contribute to student welfare in this way.

Last year, I was recognized for my involvement in the ARE community with the Scott McNiven Community Service Award. Scott passed away while in our program in 2013, and he was known for bringing people together and inspiring his peers both in and outside academia. From my nomination:

“Throughout his graduate career, Pierce has made important contributions to strengthen the social and intellectual environments of the graduate student community.”

I think some of this recognition comes from my trying to bring the ARE community closer together through social [event] planning, as it’s more noticeable. But I’ve also spent considerable time improving communication between students and faculty, two groups that assume the other is always up-to-date via some sort of osmosis. I’ve brought up items to the faculty that were previously thought of as non-issues, like the desire for STEM designation (which opens more opportunities for F-1 visa holders post-graduation). Similarly, student benefit from knowing a bit more of the reasons why department policies look like this or that; I was recently able to collect and share some information on our hiring processes (for both professors and students).

Promoting Effective Communication

I want to make the presentation of academic research more productive and less of an anxiety-ridden thing. I think this starts with a more thoughtful type of preparation. Strategies like the assertion evidence approach stress the importance of weaving slides into a narrative that can help an audience engage with material and give confidence to the speaker. Some of the outreach lectures (that were translated live!) and writing that I’ve done for general audiences have been particularly helpful for improving my communication skills.

I want to know (1) what you’re interested in and (2) why I should be too. - ARE Slam

Last year, I initiated a now-quarterly Slam-style event where students get together to practice conveying their ideas. This serves a different purpose than the standard brown-bag seminars seen in many economics departments. My goal is to provide a low-stress environment where effective communication is the sole focus. Feedback is less about the material at hand and more guidance for making clearer and more concise explanations. This becomes a huge complement to other forms of presentation practice.

The seminar has been a popular idea among my peers. You can check out the schedule for the inaugural event here. I’d be happy to talk about this if you have any questions.
