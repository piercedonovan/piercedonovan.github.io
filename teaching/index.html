Teaching

I read Surely You’re Joking, (Feynman, 1985) as a freshman physics major, and when making this site I remembered that [over-used] line about how teaching invites us to go back to things we think we know very well and wonder if there may be a better way to look at them. That changed my definition of “understanding” throughout undergrad and grad school; I feel like I spent most of my time thinking up marginally better ways to explain ideas (and constantly trying them out on peers and parents). In a formal teaching environment, I look forward to the satisfaction and understanding each new lap of ``Natural Resource Economics” brings. Below are some of the courses I’m continually developing.

Environmental and Natural Resource Economics

I don’t think we can actually understand how to properly manage the environment without economics. Economics is a discipline focused on allocating scarce resources. It provides a framework for making trade-offs between competing values, like conservation and commerce. These trade-offs help identify policy solutions for many problems, among them being natural resource management.

Here’s a copy of the syllabus and course outline for a general-interest environmental economics class at Colgate that focuses on the intuition behind the economics that drives environmental policy. I plan to prepare an upper-division natural resource economics course for the Spring that is directed towards those strong in math who want an introduction to different models in resource economics.

Statistics

I wish I had found statistics sooner. The class I teach at Colgate takes an applied approach, opting to emulate some of the lessons I’ve learned by playing with data, reading the work of creative econometricians, and watching high-quality content creators (like Numberphile or 3blue1brown). I begin each class with phenomena worth investigating and work backwards to build up the tools the class needs to examine it. No one wants to start with a t-test and then learn what it’s for. Here’s the syllabus and course outline.

Econometrics

At UC Davis, I taught an upper-division applied econometrics class. The development of the class was influenced by Angrist and Pischke’s Through Our Classes, Darkly (link). I focused on the clear, careful thinking requisite for identification of causal effects and showcased modern econometric work to meet the interests of the Managerial Economics students (which were significantly different than the interests of my [mostly engineering] peers at RIT where I first encountered a very different econometrics).

My course is featured on the Mastering ‘Metrics (Angrist and Pischke, 2014) website. For a look at that class, here’s the syllabus and course outline. Other sample materials are available by request.

Mathematical/Computational Methods [in economics]

I haven’t actually taught this one yet, but I have a pretty large folder on my computer full of potential lessons and possible directions for a class that could cover different strategies in economic modeling or numerical analysis. I’d like to one day provide students with a comprehensive tour of the lesser-used techniques available to economists. This could make a great educational complement to a modern econometrics class.

“You truly cared for this class; you answered emails at almost any times of the day, were willing to set up office hours/review times and always opened the floor for discussion.
